Recent Trends and Opportunities for Lyft Bay Wheels¶

by Jerry Yip¶

Investigation Overview¶

In this investigation, I wanted to look at growth opportunities for Lyft's operation of the Bay Wheels bike sharing system. I review lesser served groups in the context of the current ridership landscape and also analyze trends since Lyft assumed full operation of the bike sharing system from Ford in June of 2019.

Dataset Overview¶

The data consisted of rental and ridership attributes for approximately 3.7 million bike rentals on Lyft's (formerly Ford's) Bay Wheels bike sharing system from January 2018 to September 2019. The attributes included details on rental location, duration, and date-time as well as additional anonymized information on riders such as gender, birth year, and subscribership status. Nineteen data points were removed from the analysis as they corresponded to the operator's own test bike rentals.

Current Ridership Landscape¶

We start our investigation by breaking down ridership numbers across time period (time and day), demographics (gender and age), user types and associated pricing (subscriber vs customer), and then look at if and how such factors differentiate ride habits (ride duration).

Distribution of Bike Ridership by Time and Day¶

The current landscape of Bay Wheels ridership is largely comprised of rides during weekday commute hours with roughly 57% of all bike rides occur during just these times. Notice ridership is roughly twice as much on weekdays versus weekends, and that there is a bimodal distribution of bike ride times with peaks during morning and evening commute hours.

Distribution of Bike Ridership by Pricing Model¶

Lastly, we break down ridership by user type and pricing - that is, rides by monthly 'Subscribers' versus rides by one-off 'Customers'. Given a large percentage of rides are by daily commuters, we see a large percentage (84%) of rides are by monthly subscribers who pay a single monthly price for unlimited rides as compared to a small percentage (16%) of rides by non-subscribing customers who pay a small amount for each ride.

Bike Rental Duration by Pricing Model¶

Looking across the two types of bike rental pricing models (subscribership and customer), we find the average bike rental durations vary greatly (much more than for the aforementioned demographics of gender and age). Specifically, we find that non-subscriber 'Customer' rentals tend to maximize each ride as they pay the same price for up to 30 minutes, whereas subscribers ride for much shorter amounts of time — on average, the two groups average bike rentals are ~28 minutes and ~11 minutes respectively.

Growth Opportunities¶

Given the typical customer persona is a working age male who commutes to work and has a subscription membership, it suggests there are opportunities to increase ridership in two key areas:

  1. Female customers (of all age groups and user types)
  2. One-off rides (i.e. non-subscription 'Customer' rides) such as by tourists or the ad hoc user

Recent Trends: Female Ridership¶

In light of these identified opportunities, let's see how ridership has changed over time and in particular, with the transition of operations from Ford to Lyft starting in June 2019. First, we look at the breakdown of female ridership over time.

Analysis: Whereas female (as well as male) ridership was increasing year-over-year for each month in early 2019, unfortunately it looks like ridership subsequently plateaued or declined when Lyft assumed bike sharing operations in June 2019.

Conclusions: As it's only been a few months since Lyft's transition, and this same period coincided with two electric bike recalls, perhaps the verdict is still out. Furthermore, ridership by customers who do not provide their gender increased dramatically in September (likely overlaps with rental increases by non-subscribing customers), so it's too early to make a clear assessment, although there surely can be improvement in terms of Lyft pursuing this opportunity.

Recent Trends: Non-Subscription 'Customer' Rides¶

Analysis: Looking at subscription and non-subscription ridership overtime, we see a positive upward trend year-over-year for each month in ridership by non-subscribing customers. There is a small decline in May, however non-subscription ridership appears to subsequently accelerate after Lyft assumes operation from June 2019 forward.
At the same time, subscriber rides have also either increased or for the most part stayed the same year over year.

Recent Trends: Revenue Implications¶

Whereas non-subscriber rides in 2018 averaged between 30-40 minutes each month suggesting an average price of 5 dollars/ride as per both Ford's pricing model and Lyft's pricing model, those same rides in 2019 consistenly averaged less than 30 minutes, providing for an average of only 2 dollars/ride.
Meanwhile, subscription ride durations did not vary from year to year, suggesting little to no change in revenue from a per ride perspective for subscriber rentals.

Recent Trends: Revenue Implications¶

Conclusion: Given both subscriber and non-subscriber rides have increased in 2019, yet the average ride duration has stayed the same for subscribers and decreased for non-subscribers, it's not clear whether overall revenue has also increased in step with ridership numbers due to the different pricing for non-subscription rides based on duration.
At the same time, considering Lyft also provides ride share services (aka taxi and carpool service), perhaps it's in Lyft's interest to steer such customer use cases (i.e. long bike rides) towards using Lyft's other offerings instead.
